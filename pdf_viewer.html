SECTION 1 ‚Äì ACOUSTICS & THE PHYSICS OF SOUND

1.1 What is Sound?

Sound is a mechanical vibration that travels through a medium (air, water, metal) as a pressure wave. It requires three essential components:

  • a source
  • a medium
  • a receiver

Sound cannot exist in a vacuum because there is no matter to compress.

There are two main kinds of mechanical waves:

  1. Longitudinal waves: particles move back and forth along the direction of wave travel (air waves).
  2. Transverse waves: particles move perpendicular to wave travel (strings, membranes).

Human hearing typically spans from 20 Hz to 20 kHz. Below that is infrasound; above is ultrasound.

1.2 Pressure, Displacement, and Medium Mechanics

Sound travels via alternating regions of compression (high pressure) and rarefaction (low pressure). These fluctuations travel outward from the source. Individual air molecules oscillate around fixed positions‚Äîthey do not travel long distances with the wave.

The wave equation describing propagation is:

‚àÇ¬≤p/‚àÇt¬≤ = c¬≤ ‚àá¬≤p

where p is pressure, c is speed of sound, ‚àá¬≤ is the Laplacian.

Speed of sound depends on medium elasticity and density. Approximate values:

  • air: ~343 m/s
  • water: ~1480 m/s
  • steel: ~5000 m/s

1.3 Frequency, Period, and Pitch

Frequency f is measured in Hertz (cycles per second). Period T = 1/f.

Low frequency ‚Üí slow oscillation ‚Üí "bass". High frequency ‚Üí fast oscillation ‚Üí "treble".

Pitch corresponds roughly to frequency but is also influenced by harmonic structure, loudness, time evolution, and auditory system nonlinearity.

1.4 Amplitude, Loudness, and Sound Pressure Level (SPL)

Amplitude refers to maximum pressure deviation from the mean. Human loudness perception is nonlinear; we perceive loudness approximately logarithmically.

Sound pressure level:

SPL(dB) = 20 log‚ÇÅ‚ÇÄ(P / Pref) where Pref = 20 ¬µPa (threshold of hearing)

Every +6 dB ‚âà double amplitude. Every +10 dB ‚âà double perceived loudness.

1.5 Wavelength and Wave Propagation

Œª = c / f

Low frequencies ‚Üí long wavelengths ‚Üí travel far and diffract around obstacles. High frequencies ‚Üí short wavelengths ‚Üí directional and easily absorbed.

Wavelength examples in air:

  • 50 Hz ‚Üí ~6.8 m
  • 1 kHz ‚Üí ~0.34 m
  • 10 kHz ‚Üí ~0.034 m

1.6 Simple Harmonic Motion & Sinusoidal Waves

A pure tone is a sine wave:

x(t) = A sin(2œÄft + œÜ) A = amplitude f = frequency œÜ = phase

Sine waves are building blocks of all periodic signals (Fourier theory). They contain no harmonics, only one frequency.

1.7 Harmonics, Partials, and Overtones

Harmonics occur at integer multiples of a fundamental frequency f‚ÇÄ:

1st harmonic = f‚ÇÄ 2nd harmonic = 2f‚ÇÄ 3rd harmonic = 3f‚ÇÄ ...

Partials = any distinct frequency components. Overtones = partials above the fundamental.

Harmonic spectrum determines tone/timbre. Harmonic instruments include strings, brass, and woodwinds. Inharmonic instruments include bells, drums, and metal percussion.

1.8 Timbre: Why Instruments Sound Different

Timbre is determined by:

  • harmonic pattern
  • spectral envelope
  • attack/decay shape
  • presence of noise
  • inharmonicity
  • modulation and vibrato
  • time evolution of harmonics

Two instruments with the same pitch and loudness sound different because of these details.

1.9 Wave Addition, Interference, and Superposition

The principle of superposition states that wave amplitudes add linearly. Constructive interference occurs when waves add; destructive interference occurs when waves cancel.

Superposition governs beating patterns, standing waves, phase cancellation, and room resonances. If two frequencies are close, the beat frequency = |f‚ÇÅ ‚àí f‚ÇÇ|.

1.10 Standing Waves, Resonance, and Modes

Standing waves occur when reflected waves overlap with incoming ones. Nodes are areas of minimal movement; antinodes are areas of maximal movement.

Resonance occurs when excitation matches a system's natural frequency. Examples include guitar string modes, air column modes in flutes, room resonances, and timpani drum modal patterns.

Room resonances create modal peaks at:

SECTION 2 ‚Äì DIGITAL AUDIO

2.0 Introduction to Digital Audio

Digital Audio is the process of turning continuous pressure waves into numbers. Key concepts include:

  • The analog world is continuous
  • Computers are discrete
  • Conversion requires rules
  • Some things are lost, others preserved
  • Digital audio is mathematical and precise

2.1 Sampling Theory: Turning Time Into Numbers

2.1.1 What is Sampling?

Sampling means taking measurements of a waveform at regular intervals.

continuous wave ‚Üí * * * * * ‚Üí ‚Üë ‚Üë ‚Üë ‚Üë samples at fixed times

Let Fs = sampling rate (samples per second) and Ts = 1/Fs = sampling interval.

Common Fs values:

  • 44.1 kHz (music, CD)
  • 48 kHz (video, streaming)
  • 96 kHz (high-end recording)
  • 192 kHz (engineering / ultrasonic work)

2.1.2 The Nyquist Frequency

The maximum frequency that can be represented is:

Nyquist = Fs / 2

2.1.3 What Happens If Frequency > Nyquist? (Aliasing)

Aliasing occurs when high frequencies fold back into lower frequencies as wrong tones. Aliasing cannot be undone‚Äîthis is why anti-aliasing filters exist.

2.2 Quantization: Turning Amplitude Into Numbers

After sampling in time, we must also quantize amplitude. Quantization means rounding to the nearest discrete amplitude step.

2.2.1 Bit Depth

Bit depth determines how many binary digits are used per sample:

  • 16-bit (CD) ‚Üí 65,536 values
  • 24-bit (pro audio) ‚Üí 16.7 million values
  • 32-bit float ‚Üí even larger dynamic range

2.2.2 Quantization Noise

Rounding introduces error perceived as noise. More bits ‚Üí lower noise ‚Üí larger dynamic range.

Approximate dynamic range:

DR (dB) ‚âà 6.02 √ó bits

Examples: 16-bit ‚Üí ~96 dB; 24-bit ‚Üí ~144 dB

2.2.3 Dither

Dither means adding tiny noise on purpose before quantization to prevent distortion and convert quantization error into gentle noise. It's used in mastering when going from 24-bit ‚Üí 16-bit.

2.15 The Sampling Theorem (Full Intuition)

The Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem states: A bandlimited signal with no energy above Fs/2 can be perfectly reconstructed from its samples.

2.15.1 What does "bandlimited" mean?

A signal is bandlimited if X(f) = 0 for |f| > B, where B is some finite cutoff frequency.

2.15.2 Sampling = multiplying by a pulse train

Mathematically:

x_s(t) = x(t) ¬∑ Œ£ Œ¥(t ‚àí nTs)

Where Ts = 1/Fs, the sampling interval. Multiplying by impulses in time becomes replication in frequency.

2.15.3 Nyquist condition: Fs ‚â• 2B

For the replicated spectra not to overlap: Fs ‚àí B > B ‚Üí Fs > 2B. This gives us the usual condition: Fs ‚â• 2 √ó highest frequency.

SECTION 3 ‚Äì FREQUENCY DOMAIN & FFT

3.1 Time Domain vs Frequency Domain

Time domain shows amplitude as a function of time: x(t) or x[n]. Frequency domain shows amplitude/phase as a function of frequency: X(f) or X[k].

Time-domain displays loudness, waveform shape, and transients. Frequency-domain reveals pitch, harmonics, timbre, and spectral balance.

3.2 The Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT)

DFT formula:

X[k] = Œ£ x[n] ¬∑ e^(‚àíj2œÄkn/N)

Inverse DFT:

x[n] = (1/N) Œ£ X[k] ¬∑ e^(+j2œÄkn/N)

Interpretation: Each X[k] represents how much of the frequency k*(Fs/N) is in the signal. X[k] is complex = magnitude + phase.

3.3 FFT: Fast Fourier Transform

FFT is an optimized DFT, reducing computation from O(N¬≤) ‚Üí O(N log N). This enables digital EQ, convolution reverb, spectral editing, vocoders, pitch detection, and spectrograms.

FFT returns complex numbers: Re + jIm

Magnitude: |X| = sqrt(Re¬≤ + Im¬≤)

Phase: œÜ = atan2(Im, Re)

3.4 Frequency Bins & Resolution

Bin frequency:

f_bin = k * (Fs / N)

Frequency resolution:

Œîf = Fs / N

Larger N ‚Üí better frequency resolution but slower and more latency.

Example: Fs = 48000, N=2048 ‚Üí Œîf = ~23.4 Hz

3.10 STFT (Short-Time Fourier Transform)

STFT applies FFT repeatedly over overlapping windows. This creates a spectrogram showing magnitude of each frame.

Parameters include window size N, hop size H, window type, and overlap. Larger N provides better frequency resolution; smaller hop provides better time resolution. The time-frequency tradeoff is fundamental.

SECTION 4 ‚Äì DIGITAL FILTERS, SYSTEMS & SIGNAL MODELLING

4.1 Introduction to Digital Filters

A digital filter transforms a discrete input signal x[n] into an output y[n] using a difference equation.

General form:

y[n] = Œ£ b_k x[n‚àík] ‚àí Œ£ a_k y[n‚àík]

Two major classes: FIR (finite impulse response) and IIR (infinite impulse response).

Filters serve many purposes: EQ, crossover networks, smoothing, noise reduction, audio effects, and scientific DSP.

4.2 FIR Filters (Finite Impulse Response)

FIR filters have finite-length impulse responses.

General FIR equation:

y[n] = Œ£ b_k x[n‚àík]

Properties:

  • always stable
  • can be linear phase
  • direct convolution interpretation

Design methods include windowed-sinc, frequency sampling, Parks‚ÄìMcClellan (equiripple), and least-squares filter design.

4.4 IIR Filters (Infinite Impulse Response)

IIR filters use feedback in their difference equations:

y[n] = Œ£ b_k x[n‚àík] ‚àí Œ£ a_k y[n‚àík]

Properties:

  • very efficient
  • sharp frequency response
  • can become unstable
  • non-linear phase

Common designs include Butterworth (smooth), Chebyshev I & II (steeper), Elliptic (steepest), and Biquads (2nd-order sections).

4.5 Biquad Filters (2nd-Order IIR Sections)

Biquad filters are standard in audio DSP.

Structure:

y[n] = b0 x[n] + b1 x[n‚àí1] + b2 x[n‚àí2] ‚àí a1 y[n‚àí1] ‚àí a2 y[n‚àí2]

Used for parametric EQ, low/high shelf, peaking EQ, low-pass, high-pass, notch filters, and band-pass filtering.

SECTION 5 ‚Äì SOUND SYNTHESIS & TIMBRE MODELLING

5.1 Fundamentals of Sound Synthesis

Synthesis means generating sound using mathematical or physical models. Main synthesis categories include:

  • Additive
  • Subtractive
  • FM/PM
  • Wavetable
  • Physical modeling
  • Granular
  • Sampling

5.2 Additive Synthesis

Additive synthesis is based on summing sinusoidal partials:

x(t) = Œ£ A_k(t) ¬∑ sin(2œÄ f_k(t) t + œÜ_k(t))

Parameters include amplitudes, frequencies, and phases. Models can be harmonic additive, inharmonic additive, or use time-varying envelopes.

Used for organ sounds, bells, pads, and spectral morphing.

5.3 Subtractive Synthesis

Subtractive synthesis starts with harmonically rich waveforms (saw, square, triangle, or noise) and then shapes them with filters (low-pass, band-pass, formant filters, or resonators).

Typical subtractive synth path:

Oscillator ‚Üí Filter ‚Üí Amplifier ‚Üí Output

Modulation sources include LFOs, envelopes, key tracking, and velocity.

5.4 FM Synthesis (Frequency Modulation)

FM synthesis uses a carrier and modulator:

Carrier: c(t) = sin(2œÄ f_c t) Modulator: m(t) = sin(2œÄ f_m t) FM output: y = sin(2œÄ f_c t + I sin(2œÄ f_m t))

Where I = modulation index (controls brightness). This produces sidebands at frequencies f_c ¬± n f_m.

Used for electric piano, metallic tones, bells, and complex evolving timbre.

5.6 Wavetable Synthesis

A table stores a cycle of a waveform in buffer[n]. A phase accumulator picks values:

phase += freq/Fs output = buffer[floor(phase * table_size)]

Features include morphing between tables, multi-dimensional tables, and anti-aliased tables. Used in Serum, Vital, Massive, and PPG Wave synthesizers.

SECTION 7 ‚Äì MIXING, MASTERING, EFFECTS & REAL-WORLD AUDIO

7.1 Gain Staging & Levels

Digital audio has a strict range: -1.0 to +1.0 (float), where 0 dBFS = maximum digital amplitude.

Gain staging ensures no clipping, no noise floor issues, and that plugins operate optimally.

Recommended practice:

  • leave 6‚Äì12 dB headroom on tracks
  • leave 6 dB on master
  • peaks under ‚àí6 dBFS

7.3 Compression

Compression reduces dynamic range. Parameters include ratio, threshold, attack, release, knee, and makeup gain.

Basic formula:

if x > T: y = T + (x‚àíT)/R else y = x

Types of compressors include VCA (fast, precise), Opto (smooth), FET (aggressive), Vari-Mu (tube, warm), and Digital lookahead.

Use-cases include vocals, drums, bass, buses, and glue compression.

7.7 Reverb

Reverb simulates acoustic space. Main components include early reflections, late reverberation, and decay time (RT60).

Reverb types:

  • convolution (uses IRs)
  • algorithmic (Schroeder, FDN)
  • plate
  • spring
  • chamber
  • hall

Reverb controls include pre-delay, size, damping, mix, and diffusion.

SECTION 10 ‚Äì MACHINE LEARNING FOR AUDIO, MUSIC & DSP

10.1 Audio Features for Machine Learning

ML models typically work with audio representations rather than raw audio. Common features include:

  • STFT magnitude
  • log-mel spectrogram
  • mel-frequency cepstral coefficients (MFCCs)
  • chroma features
  • spectral centroid / bandwidth / rolloff / flux
  • zero-crossing rate
  • spectral envelope

Log-mel spectrogram is the standard for ML:

audio ‚Üí STFT ‚Üí mel filterbank ‚Üí log(energy)

10.2 Deep Learning for Audio

Neural architectures used in audio processing:

CNNs (Convolutional Neural Networks)

Used for spectrogram classification, instrument recognition, sound effects classification, pitch estimation, and spectrogram enhancement. CNN filters act like temporal filters and time-frequency pattern detectors.

RNNs (Recurrent Neural Networks)

Used for music generation, sequence modeling, and speech recognition. Variants include LSTM and GRU.

Transformers (Attention Models)

Now the most powerful sequence models. Used for large-scale music modeling, audio understanding, source separation, and multi-modal models (audio + text).

Self-attention determines global relationships across entire audio sequences.

10.7 Neural Synthesis (DDSP, GANs, Diffusion)

Modern synthesis uses ML to generate or transform sounds.

DDSP (Differentiable DSP)

Uses differentiable filters and oscillators. The model learns control signals for DSP modules, providing extremely high quality. Used for voice conversion & instrument reconstruction.

GAN-based synthesis

Adversarial training generates textures, drum hits, and transients.

Diffusion Models

Current state-of-the-art includes AudioLDM, DiffWave, and Stable Audio. Process: noise ‚Üí iterative denoise ‚Üí audio.

SECTION 12 ‚Äì PRACTICAL IMPLEMENTATION & SYSTEM DESIGN

12.1 Real-Time Audio Constraints

Real-time audio processing has strict requirements:

  • fixed processing time per buffer
  • no buffer overruns
  • no dynamic allocation
  • no blocking operations
  • deterministic execution
  • zero dropouts

Audio callback must:

process(input, output, N samples)

within:

N / Fs seconds

Example:

  • Fs = 48000
  • buffer = 64 samples
  • time available = 1.33 ms

All DSP must finish before this deadline.

12.2 Audio Thread vs Non-Real-Time Threads

Audio applications typically have:

  • audio thread (real-time, highest priority)
  • GUI thread (non-real-time)
  • background threads (file loading, ML inference, etc.)

Audio thread rules:

  • no lock()
  • no malloc/free
  • no disk I/O
  • no sleep()
  • no waiting
  • no logging
  • no virtual calls

12.3 Block Processing & Buffering

DSP is done in blocks:

  • block size N
  • overlap-save or overlap-add for convolution
  • circular buffers for delay lines

Circular buffer index:

idx = (idx + 1) % buffer_size

Block diagram:

input ‚Üí process block ‚Üí output

12.4 Latency & Buffer Size

Latency:

latency = (buffer size) / sample rate + hardware delays

Trade-offs:

  • small buffers ‚Üí low latency, high CPU load
  • large buffers ‚Üí high latency, stable processing

Live performance requires:

< 5 ms round-trip latency
